Anti-Drugs Movement
 First, a narrative that many families may identify with... When a typical middle-class family discovers that their child is a drug addict, they are devastated. The family is understandably concerned and distressed. The family does what any decent family would do: they seek treatment at a rehabilitation facility. However, the family discovers that the cost is well beyond their means. The question now is, what should they do?
 According to available data, over 150,000 persons in Nepal consume drugs, with 52% of them being between the ages of 15 and 24. Since 2007, the number of drug users has more than doubled. The majority of people who get hooked to drugs did not do so on purpose. Abusers who are desperate for drugs may turn aggressive. As a result, drug use encourages criminal behavior. Drug abusers are believed to conduct more than 50 million crimes worldwide each year. "Under the influence of intoxicants, a man who might normally be controlled might lose self-control, become thoughtless, and participate in murdering, theft, adultery, and lying," remarked Bhikkhu Bodhi, a world-famous Buddhist monk of the Theravada school.

So, what are the options?
  To begin, the government should have a clear vision with a specific purpose within a certain time frame in order to establish a strong national drug policy. We require a policy that addresses all aspects of drug use, including health, education, and criminality. Family care, community mobilization, and rehabilitation must all be balanced in the policy.
